Case Study — Platform Design

Financial Finesse Hub

Redesigned a financial wellness platform from the ground up — shipped as a fully functional demo in two months

Financial Finesse delivers employer-sponsored financial coaching to Fortune 500 workforces. Their existing platform had accumulated years of feature debt — outdated navigation, an inconsistent component library, and a fragmented employee experience. I led a full redesign from discovery through a high-fidelity functional demo.

The Problem

A platform with great content that nobody used — and great coaching no one could find.

The Hub had years of high-quality financial content and access to certified CFPs. The experience made both nearly impossible to discover.

Legacy Financial Finesse Hub — Dashboard
TODO → images/hub-before.jpg (16:9 ratio, show the old fragmented platform experience)

What employees experienced

A generic dashboard with no clear next step.

  • Navigation had 14+ top-level items with no prioritization — employees didn't know where to start
  • No personalization — every employee saw the same homepage regardless of their financial situation
  • CFP access was buried 3–4 clicks deep in a platform built around tools, not people
  • Mobile experience was an afterthought — causing drop-off on smaller screens

What it cost the business

Engagement metrics telling the wrong story.

  • Employer clients measured ROI in session depth and return visits — both were plateauing
  • Sales was losing deals to competitors with more modern UX, even when Financial Finesse had objectively better content
  • The design language was inconsistent across modules built over 8+ years — engineering spent disproportionate time on UI bugs

How Might We

The questions that shaped our redesign strategy.

Before we touched a pixel, we aligned on the right questions. These frames kept us from designing solutions in search of problems.

01

How might we help an employee know exactly what to do next — the moment they open the platform?

02

How might we put the CFP relationship — not the feature library — at the center of the experience?

03

How might we build a design system in week one so we can build screens in week four?

04

How might we create a demo that sells itself — without needing a salesperson to explain what they're looking at?

The Solution

A personalized wellness platform that leads with people, not features.

The redesigned Hub surfaces each employee's CFP, open goals, and next recommended action on load. Navigation collapsed to five core areas. Every screen built from a consistent design system shipped in week two.

01

The design system pays for itself — but only if you build it in week one.

The first two weeks felt slow because I was building tokens and components instead of screens. By week three, I was composing full pages faster than I ever could have screen-by-screen. If I'd started with screens, the second half of the project would have been chaos. The investment in system-first design was the single biggest accelerant on this project.

02

Engagement is a structural problem, not a content problem.

The platform had excellent content but poor engagement. The issue was always architectural — how information was organized, surfaced, and sequenced — not what was inside it. Personalization at entry changed everything downstream. An employee who tells you their top financial priority on day one will have a fundamentally different relationship with the platform than one who was shown a generic home screen.
